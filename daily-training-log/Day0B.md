## Key Takeaways
- Edge can come from finding a pattern in price/volatility/volume that slightly predicts future returns (better than completely random chance), learning about various structural effects (which I'll look into later), or even just having better pricing and/or execution (getting slightly better prices on average/lower transaction costs than a naive trader)  
- Edge needs to be non-trivial in practice, since markets have lots of friction coming from various sources like commissions, bid-ask spreads, slippage, and even just bad luck streaks which can all go a long way. In other words, as a quant trader, edge needs to be statistically significant (not random noise in backtests) while being large enough to survive trading costs or capacity limits
- It's important to always have your position (long/short) and whether or not you predict the price to move up/down in mind when adjusting prices (obviously but it was worth mentioning)
- Unlike prop shops, where market makers earn profits from the bid-ask spread that they set and very high trading volume, hedge fund QTs try to predict price movements and make directional bets based on those predictions (so generally less frequent trades, and they don't act as market makers). Relies on mispricings within the market (where EV of an asset/stock deviates from the market value). Managing risk is really important to prevent bad outcomes from wiping away the profits (when the model is wrong).
